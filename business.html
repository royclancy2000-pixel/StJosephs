Pillar I — Business & Entrepreneurship

Men who can build, employ, and provide.

This is the pillar that sets us apart. We do not merely teach boys to find a job; we form them to create value, take ownership, and answer for what they build — in the light of faith.

Why Enterprise Leads

The forgotten part of forming a man

A young man can graduate able to parse Virgil and serve at the altar, and still not know how to read a balance sheet, price his labor, or build something that lasts. We think that is a gap worth closing — deliberately, and at the center of his education.

To create a business is to imitate, in a small and human way, the generosity of the Creator: to bring something into being that did not exist before, to make it useful to others, and to take responsibility for it. Done rightly, enterprise is not greed dressed up in a suit. It is stewardship with sleeves rolled up — the daily work of providing for a family, employing a neighbor, strengthening a community, and turning God-given talents into real and lasting fruit.

So we put it first. Not above the altar — nothing stands above the altar — but first among the things we teach a boy to do in the world. Every other pillar pours into it. Latin disciplines his reasoning. The farm teaches him that value comes from work. The chapel reminds him whom he ultimately serves. And the business program gives all of that a place to become real.

"Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little; I will set you over much."
Matthew 25:23
The Curriculum

From the ledger to the launch

The enterprise seminar runs every year a boy is with us, deepening as he matures — from the first principles of money to the running of a real venture.

Financial Literacy & Accounting

Every graduate leaves able to keep books, read an income statement and balance sheet, build a budget, understand interest and debt, and tell the difference between profit and cash. We start with the language of money so that nothing about it intimidates him later.

Running a Real Enterprise

Theory is cheap. Our boys operate actual ventures with real customers, real revenue, and real consequences — the farm as a managed P&L, a campus store, and student-led trades and services. They feel the weight of a payroll and the satisfaction of a sale.

Pitching & Presenting

A man who cannot make his case will watch lesser ideas win. Boys learn to write a one-page plan, model the numbers, stand before a room, and defend their thinking under questioning — culminating each year in a pitch before a panel of Catholic business mentors.

Ownership & the Moral Life of Money

We teach the hard questions alongside the spreadsheets: What is a just wage? What do I owe my workers, my customers, and the poor? When is debt prudent and when is it a trap? Money is a good servant and a cruel master, and we form men to keep it in its place.

Markets, Trades & Hard Skills

Carpentry, repair, basic construction, and the practical trades sit beside the business theory. A boy who can fix the thing he sells, and build the thing he imagines, negotiates the world from a position of competence and strength.

Mentorship from Catholic Builders

Faithful entrepreneurs, founders, and tradesmen come to campus to advise, critique, and walk alongside our boys. They show, by their own lives, that a man can be devout and ambitious, generous and successful, all at once.

The Flagship Venture

The farm is a real business — and the boys run it

Our working farm is not a hobby plot or a science fair. It is a genuine operation with a profit-and-loss statement, and students help manage it as such.

They forecast the season, set prices for eggs, meat, and produce, manage costs and inventory, staff the weekend market stand, and reconcile the books at month's end. When a hard winter raises feed costs, they feel it in the margin and have to respond. When the market stand has a record Saturday, they know exactly why.

It is the most honest classroom we have: the land does not grade on a curve, and the customer does not care about your excuses. See the Farm & Land program →

Illustrative Student Ventures

The kinds of enterprises a boy might build here

Examples of the real, revenue-generating ventures our program is designed to launch and sustain. Final ventures will reflect each class's gifts and the needs of the school.

The Schola Farm Market

Eggs, pork, seasonal produce, and preserves sold to the surrounding community at a weekend stand and by subscription. The anchor enterprise — and the school's most public face.

The Campus Store & Coffee Bar

A student-managed shop selling crest goods, sundries, and roasted coffee to families on visiting days. A first lesson in retail, inventory, and customer service.

The Woodshop & Repair Co.

Custom furniture, rosaries, cutting boards, and small repairs — turning the carpentry curriculum into a maker's business with a real order book.

Grounds & Trades Services

A seasonal crew offering fencing, mowing, and small-job labor to neighbors — teaching boys to bid work, schedule, and stand behind a job well done.

Sacred Sound Recordings

Recording and selling the schola's chant and sacred music. Where the music pillar meets the marketplace, and beauty helps sustain the mission.

The Apiary & Honey Label

Beekeeping, harvesting, and a small branded honey line — a tidy, teachable enterprise in production, packaging, and pricing.
