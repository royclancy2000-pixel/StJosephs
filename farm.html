Pillar IV — Farming & the Land

Where work becomes wisdom.

A genuine working farm that teaches a boy responsibility, stewardship, and the plain truth that value comes from labor — and that turns a real profit doing it.

Formation Through Labor

The land does not negotiate

There is no faking it on a farm. The animals must be fed whether or not you feel like it. The fence must be mended before the cattle are out, not after. The harvest comes on its own schedule. A boy learns more about responsibility in one season of chores than in a year of lectures about it.

Our farm is woven into the daily rhythm of the school. Before Mass, the boys are in the barn. After the afternoon's lessons, they are in the fields and the workshop. They raise the animals, tend the crops, mend what breaks, and bring the harvest to table and to market. In doing so they acquire the old, unglamorous virtues — diligence, patience, reliability, and care for living things — that no amount of classroom instruction can supply.

The Program

What happens on the land

animal-husbandry-barn.jpg

Animal Husbandry

Cattle, pigs, poultry, and bees — raised humanely and well. Boys learn feeding, health, breeding, and the daily care that livestock demand.

crops-and-harvest.jpg

Crops & Growing

Seasonal vegetables, grains, and an orchard. The boys plant, weed, water, and harvest, learning the rhythm of the growing year firsthand.

farm-to-table-kitchen.jpg

Farm to Table

Much of what the school eats is grown or raised here. Boys see the whole arc from soil to supper and learn that good food is the fruit of good work.

land-stewardship-fencing.jpg

Stewardship of the Land

Soil health, rotation, water, and conservation. We treat the land as a trust to be handed on better than we found it — a Catholic ecology of care.

trades-workshop.jpg

Trades & Maintenance

Carpentry, fencing, small engines, and repair. The farm is a workshop where boys gain the hands-on competence that makes a man useful anywhere.

weekend-market-stand.jpg

The Market Stand

Where the farm meets the customer. Boys run the weekend stand and subscription sales — the front line of the school's flagship enterprise.

farm-as-business-ledger.jpg
The Farm as a Teaching Business

A classroom with a bottom line

What sets our farm apart is that it is run, in part, by the students as a real enterprise — with revenue, costs, and a margin that must be defended.

The farm is where our first and fourth pillars meet. Boys do not just feed the pigs; they track the cost of feed against the price of pork. They do not just sell eggs; they set the price, manage the inventory, and reconcile the till. The farm gives the business program a living, breathing case study that changes with the weather and rewards good judgment with real results.
